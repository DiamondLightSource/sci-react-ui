  <h1>Colour contrast</h1>
<small>Updated 23 March 2026</small>

<p>
  Colour contrast directly affects readability, speed, and error rate. In dense,
  data-heavy scientific interfaces, poor contrast increases cognitive load and
  slows decision-making.
</p>

<p>
  We prioritise perceptual contrast (APCA) for real-world readability, while
  maintaining WCAG 2.x as a compliance baseline.
</p>

<h2>Why contrast needs a modern approach</h2>

<p>
  Traditional contrast ratios (WCAG 2.x) are based on simplified luminance
  calculations. They do not account for how people actually see, especially
  across different font sizes, weights, and light/dark environments.
</p>

<p>
  This leads to common issues:
</p>

<ul>
  <li>Combinations that “pass” but feel hard to read.</li>
  <li>Overly strong contrast that causes eye strain in dark mode.</li>
  <li>Poor performance with saturated colours (green, orange, red).</li>
</ul>

<p>
  APCA addresses these limitations by modelling perceived contrast instead of
  raw luminance difference.
</p>

<h2>APCA contrast (primary)</h2>

<p>
  APCA (Advanced Perceptual Contrast Algorithm) reflects how contrast is
  actually experienced by users.
</p>

<ul>
  <li>Accounts for font size, weight, and spatial frequency.</li>
  <li>Handles polarity correctly (light-on-dark vs dark-on-light).</li>
  <li>Produces more reliable results for real UI text.</li>
  <li>Reduces eye strain in long-running workflows.</li>
</ul>

<p>
  We use APCA to define token values, component defaults, and interaction states.
</p>

<h2>WCAG 2.x contrast (baseline)</h2>

<p>
  WCAG 2.x contrast ratios remain the current regulatory standard.
</p>

<ul>
  <li>Defines minimum acceptable contrast thresholds.</li>
  <li>Used as a strict “do not go below” constraint.</li>
  <li>Required for compliance and accessibility audits.</li>
</ul>

<p>
  However, WCAG 2.x should not be used alone to judge readability quality.
</p>

<h2>How we use them together</h2>

<p>
  Contrast decisions follow a clear order:
</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>1. Design using APCA</strong> for perceptual readability.</li>
  <li><strong>2. Validate against WCAG</strong> for compliance.</li>
  <li><strong>3. Adjust only if required</strong>, without degrading usability.</li>
</ul>

<p>
  This ensures interfaces are both compliant and genuinely usable.
</p>

  <div>
    <h2>Colour-specific considerations (green, orange, and red)</h2>

    <p>
      Some hues behave poorly for perceived contrast, even when WCAG 2 ratios pass.
    </p>

    <p>
      Green, orange, and red are particularly sensitive due to luminance behaviour,
      human vision response, and colour vision differences.
    </p>

    <p>
      A common issue in real interfaces: WCAG 2 may suggest black text on saturated
      colours is “high contrast”, but visually it can feel unstable, vibrating, or
      tiring, especially in small or dense text.
    </p>

    <p>
      APCA more accurately reflects these perception issues.
    </p>

    <ul>
      <li>
        <strong>Green</strong>: can appear muddy or unstable with dark text,
        especially at smaller sizes.
      </li>
      <li>
        <strong>Orange</strong>: often lacks perceived depth, reducing clarity on
        both light and dark surfaces.
      </li>
      <li>
        <strong>Red</strong>: prone to “vibration” effects and reduced legibility,
        particularly in error states.
      </li>
    </ul>

    <p>
      When using these colours:
    </p>

    <ul>
      <li>Use APCA to validate readability, not just WCAG ratios.</li>
      <li>Test across sizes, weights, and themes.</li>
      <li>Always pair with text, icons, or labels.</li>
    </ul>

    <p><strong>WCAG 2 vs APCA: why “passes” can still fail users</strong></p>
    <p>
      WCAG 2 is a useful baseline, but it does not reliably predict readability.
      APCA aligns more closely with how users actually perceive contrast.
    </p>

<h2>Practical guidance</h2>
<ul>
  <li>
    <strong>Body text</strong>: optimise for APCA readability first, then confirm WCAG.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>UI chrome</strong>: maintain clear separation and predictable hierarchy.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Subtle text</strong>: only for non-essential information.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Disabled states</strong>: must remain distinguishable without becoming unreadable.
  </li>
</ul>

<h2>Common pitfalls</h2>
<ul>
  <li>Relying on WCAG ratios alone to judge readability.</li>
  <li>Reducing contrast for visual “subtlety” where meaning is still required.</li>
  <li>Using saturated colours without perceptual validation.</li>
  <li>Ignoring polarity differences between light and dark mode.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When WCAG and APCA disagree</h2>
<ul>
  <li>If WCAG fails, the colour must change (non-compliant).</li>
  <li>
    If WCAG passes but APCA indicates poor readability, adjust the colour to improve perceptual contrast - WCAG compliance alone is not sufficient.
  </li>
  <li>
    In critical workflows, readability and accuracy always take precedence over visual subtlety.
  </li>
</ul>

<h2>Future: WCAG 3 and APCA</h2>

<p>
  WCAG 3 is moving towards perceptual contrast models, with APCA as a core
  component. This reflects a broader shift from mathematical ratios to
  human-centred readability.
</p>

<p>
  The exact contrast method is still evolving in the WCAG 3 draft, but current
  direction and research strongly favour perceptual approaches such as APCA.
</p>

<p>
  Our approach reflects where standards are going, not just where they are today:
</p>

<ul>
  <li>APCA defines how we design and tune contrast.</li>
  <li>WCAG 2.x ensures compliance during the transition period.</li>
  <li>
    This reduces future rework as WCAG 3 becomes the formal standard.
  </li>
</ul>
