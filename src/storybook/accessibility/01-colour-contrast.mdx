<div>
  <h1>Colour contrast</h1>
  <small>Updated 28 March 2026</small>

  <p>Colour contrast directly affects readability, speed, and error rate. In dense, data-heavy scientific interfaces, poor contrast increases cognitive load and slows decision-making.</p>

  <p>We prioritise perceptual contrast using APCA when defining colour choices, while maintaining WCAG 2.2 as the compliance baseline.</p>

  <h2>Why contrast needs a modern approach</h2>

  <p>Traditional WCAG 2.x contrast ratios are useful, but they are based on luminance ratios rather than perceived readability. In practice, that means some combinations can technically pass while still feeling strained or unstable in real UI.</p>

  <p>This becomes more visible across different font sizes, weights, and light or dark environments.</p>

  <p>This leads to common issues:</p>

  <ul>
    <li>Combinations that pass but still feel hard to read.</li>
    <li>Overly strong contrast that causes glare or eye strain in dark mode.</li>
    <li>Poor readability with saturated colours such as success, warning, and danger.</li>
  </ul>

  <p>APCA helps address these limitations by modelling perceived readability rather than relying only on raw luminance difference.</p>

  <h2>APCA contrast (primary)</h2>

  <p>APCA reflects how contrast is actually experienced by users more closely than older ratio-based methods.</p>

  <ul>
    <li>Accounts for font size, weight, and polarity.</li>
    <li>Handles light-on-dark and dark-on-light differently.</li>
    <li>Produces more reliable results for real UI text.</li>
    <li>Helps reduce strain in long-running workflows.</li>
  </ul>

  <p>We use APCA to tune token values, component defaults, and interaction states.</p>

  <h2>WCAG 2.2 contrast (baseline)</h2>

  <p>WCAG 2.2 remains the current compliance baseline for text contrast.</p>

  <ul>
    <li>Defines minimum acceptable contrast thresholds.</li>
    <li>Used as a strict floor we do not go below.</li>
    <li>Required for accessibility reviews and audits.</li>
  </ul>

  <p>WCAG 2.2 is necessary, but on its own it is not enough to judge readability quality.</p>

  <h2>How we use them together</h2>

  <p>Contrast decisions follow a clear order:</p>

  <ul>
    <li><strong>1. Design using APCA</strong> for perceptual readability.</li>
    <li><strong>2. Validate against WCAG 2.2</strong> for compliance.</li>
    <li><strong>3. Adjust only if required</strong>, without degrading usability.</li>
  </ul>

  <p>This helps keep interfaces both compliant and genuinely usable.</p>

    <p>In DiamondDS this matters most for status colours: <code>--ds-success</code>, <code>--ds-warning</code>, and <code>--ds-danger</code>.</p>

    <p>A common issue in real interfaces is that black text on a saturated colour may look mathematically strong, but visually feel unstable, vibrating, or tiring, especially for smaller or denser text.</p>

    <p>APCA is better at exposing these perception problems.</p>

    <ul>
      <li><strong>Success</strong>: can feel muddy or uneven with dark text, especially in small labels.</li>
      <li><strong>Warning</strong>: often looks shallower than expected, reducing clarity on both light and dark surfaces.</li>
      <li><strong>Danger</strong>: more prone to vibration and reduced legibility, particularly in error messaging.</li>
    </ul>

    <p>When using these colours:</p>

    <ul>
      <li>Use APCA to judge readability, not just WCAG 2.2 ratios.</li>
      <li>Test across sizes, weights, and both themes.</li>
      <li>Always pair colour with text, icons, or labels.</li>

  <h2>Practical guidance</h2>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Body text</strong>: optimise for perceptual readability first, then confirm WCAG 2.2 compliance.</li>
    <li><strong>UI chrome</strong>: maintain clear separation and predictable hierarchy through surface and border roles.</li>
    <li><strong>Subtle text</strong>: only use for non-essential information.</li>
    <li><strong>Disabled states</strong>: must remain distinguishable without becoming unreadable.</li>
  </ul>

  <h2>Common pitfalls</h2>
  <ul>
    <li>Relying on WCAG ratios alone to judge readability.</li>
    <li>Reducing contrast for visual subtlety where meaning is still required.</li>
    <li>Using saturated colours without perceptual validation.</li>
    <li>Ignoring polarity differences between light and dark mode.</li>
  </ul>

  <h2>When WCAG and APCA disagree</h2>
  <ul>
    <li>If WCAG 2.2 fails, the colour must change.</li>
    <li>If WCAG 2.2 passes but APCA still indicates poor readability, improve the colour choice rather than treating compliance as enough.</li>
    <li>In critical workflows, readability and accuracy take precedence over visual subtlety.</li>
  </ul>

  <h2>Future: WCAG 3 and APCA</h2>

  <p>WCAG 3 is still evolving, but perceptual contrast models such as APCA are influencing the direction of accessibility guidance.</p>

  <p>Our approach reflects that direction while staying grounded in current compliance requirements:</p>

  <ul>
    <li>Use perceptual contrast thinking to design and tune colour choices.</li>
    <li>Validate text contrast against WCAG 2.2 today.</li>
    <li>Reduce future rework by avoiding colour decisions that are only mathematically compliant.</li>
